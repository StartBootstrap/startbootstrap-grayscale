What is a Biosphere Reserve?

Biosphere reserves combine conservation of biodiversity with sustainability. Each of the 714 biosphere reserves in the world impacts the lives of 250 million people because of the combination of their unique ecological, social and economic contexts. Each of these sites are “learning places for sustainable development.”

A Brief History of the UNESCO Biosphere Classification

There are 46 North American sites that are currently classified by UNESCO as Biosphere Reserves. This classification originated in 1968 during a UNESCO-organized intergovernmental conference concerned with conserving natural resources. Two years after this conference, in 1970, UNESCO introduced the "Man and the Biosphere" program, which sought to establish a world network of sites to protect ecosystems with significant diversity in resources. While Reserve sites remain under national jurisdiction, the World Network of Biosphere Reserves is an information and training network for understanding and maintaining ecological and cultural diversity.

Active Biospheres

Active biosphere reserves must meet three complementary and mutually reinforcing functions.

  • The main areas form a protective ecosystem that contribures to the conseration of the ecosystem, landscape, genetic and species variation.
  • The buffer zone around the main area is used for activities that are compatiable with sound ecological practices and scientific study.
  • The transition area is where the most activity is allowed and fosters a sustainable human and ecological relationship.

Removed Biospheres

It is not unusual for biospheres to be withdrawned. Often it is because there is a lack of a human population around the reserves or lack of local support. In 2017 the United States withdrew 17 biosphere reserves, but plan to rejoin the at a later date.
